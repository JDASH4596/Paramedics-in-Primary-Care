Paediatric Assessment

PIPC quick reference

Initial Impression (Paediatric Assessment Triangle)

Appearance
  • Tone / interaction
  • Consolability
  • Awake vs drowsy
Work of breathing
  • Recession / nasal flaring
  • Grunting / head bobbing
  • Audible wheeze/stridor
Circulation to skin
  • Pallor / mottling / cyanosis
  • Cap refill trend
  • Temperature of peripheries

If PAT suggests “unwell child”, move straight to ABCDE and escalate early.

Primary Survey (ABCDE)

A – Airway

  • Stridor, drooling, muffled voice
  • Unable to swallow / tripod position
  • Foreign body risk

B – Breathing

  • RR, recession, nasal flaring, grunting
  • SpO₂, auscultation, symmetry

C – Circulation

  • HR, cap refill, skin colour/temperature
  • Hydration (wet nappies/urine, tears, mucosa)

D – Disability

  • AVPU / GCS, irritability vs lethargy
  • Seizure activity, glucose if indicated

E – Exposure

  • Temperature, rash (incl non-blanching), trauma
  • Safeguarding concerns

ABCDE ENTT + Tummy Examination

A – Airway

  • Voice change, drooling, stridor
  • Trismus, swallowing difficulty

B – Breathing

  • Observe WOB (recession/flaring/grunting)
  • Consider upper-airway causes (e.g., croup)

C – Circulation

  • Perfusion, hydration, fever, tachycardia
  • Consider sepsis if “toxic” appearance

D – Detailed ENTT + Tummy

Ears
  • TM: redness / bulging / effusion
  • Discharge, mastoid tenderness
Nose
  • Discharge, foreign body, septal swelling
  • Sinus tenderness (older children)
Throat
  • Tonsils: exudate, asymmetry
  • Uvula deviation, soft palate swelling (PTA concern)
Tummy
  • Distension, focal tenderness, guarding, masses
  • Hydration clues: dry mucosa, reduced urine, poor intake

Paediatric Early Warning Scores

  • PEWS: Use local Paediatric Early Warning Score chart to trend deterioration over time.
  • POPS: Consider Paediatric Observation Priority Score in urgent care/ED style assessment to support disposition.
  • Escalate for senior review if score rises, abnormal observations persist, or clinical concern is high regardless of score.

Common Paediatric Conditions (quick guide)

Croup management
  • Barking cough/stridor; keep child calm, avoid upsetting throat exam.
  • Single-dose oral dexamethasone where indicated; urgent escalation for stridor at rest, hypoxia or exhaustion.
Viral wheeze
  • Episode linked to viral URTI, often in younger children.
  • Assess response to bronchodilator trial and work of breathing; escalate if poor response or persistent hypoxia.
Bronchiolitis
  • Usually under 2 years: coryza then cough, crackles/wheeze, increased work of breathing.
  • Supportive care focus (feeding/hydration/oxygen need); refer if apnoea, poor feeding, dehydration, or respiratory distress.
Scarlet fever / Strep A
  • Fever, sore throat, sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue, cervical nodes.
  • Use local/national antimicrobial guidance and strict safety-netting for invasive Group A Strep red flags.
Viral rashes
  • Most are self-limiting if child is systemically well and rash blanches.
  • Urgent action for non-blanching rash, toxic appearance, meningism, breathing compromise, or dehydration.

Red Flags

  • Non-blanching rash
  • Reduced consciousness, inconsolable or “too sleepy”
  • Apnoea / severe respiratory distress
  • Seizure (esp new/prolonged)
  • Bilious vomiting
  • Bulging fontanelle (infants)
  • Severe dehydration (no urine / very dry / shock signs)

Key History (fast)

  • Duration + trajectory (“better, worse, same?”)
  • Feeding + fluids
  • Wet nappies / urine output
  • Breathing: work, pauses, noisy breathing
  • Vomiting/diarrhoea, rash
  • Vaccinations, sick contacts
  • PMH (prematurity, cardiac/resp disease), meds/allergies
