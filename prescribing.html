Simple Prescribing: KISS Checklist

A quick, practical guide for safer prescribing in Primary Care. Keep it simple: use the right medicine, for the right person, at the lowest effective dose, for the shortest appropriate time.

10-second safety pause before you prescribe

  1. Do they actually need a medicine today?
  2. Any red flags, allergy, pregnancy, frailty, renal/liver issues?
  3. Could this medicine worsen falls, confusion, constipation, or drowsiness?
  4. Start low, go slow, and set a clear review date.

1) Core best practice (every prescription)

  • Confirm diagnosis or likely working diagnosis first.
  • Check current meds to avoid duplication and interactions.
  • Use one clear goal: symptom relief, prevention, or cure.
  • Document indication, dose, duration, and review plan.
  • Safety-net: what to do if worse, no better, or side effects occur.

2) Lowest effective dose: start low, go slow

  • Use the minimum effective starting dose whenever possible.
  • Titrate only if needed and only after checking benefit and harm.
  • Older adults often need lower starting doses.
  • Avoid multiple medicine changes at once where possible.
  • Review early after starting: effect, side effects, adherence.

3) ACB + falls in older adults

  • Anticholinergic burden (ACB) can cause confusion, dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, and falls.
  • Before adding medicines, check if current meds already raise ACB.
  • Be extra cautious with sedating or hypotensive medicines in people with falls risk.
  • If risk is high, consider non-drug options or safer alternatives first.
  • After prescribing, ask about dizziness, drowsiness, delirium, and near-falls at review.

4) Antibiotic stewardship (KISS)

  • Only prescribe antibiotics when bacterial infection is likely and benefit is clear.
  • Use local/national guideline choice, dose, and shortest effective duration.
  • Avoid antibiotics for likely viral self-limiting illness.
  • Consider delayed/back-up prescribing where appropriate.
  • Give clear advice on red flags, expected illness length, and re-consult triggers.

5) Practical checks before signing

  • Right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right duration.
  • Check renal/hepatic function if relevant to dosing.
  • Check interactions with anticoagulants, opioids, CNS depressants, and OTC/herbal products.
  • Use plain language: when and how to take it, and common side effects.
  • Add review/stop date for short-term or high-risk medicines.

6) Deprescribing mindset

  • At each review, ask: is this medicine still needed?
  • Stop ineffective, duplicate, or harmful treatment.
  • Taper medicines that need gradual withdrawal.
  • Prioritise reducing medicines that increase falls, confusion, and hospital risk.

Educational quick-reference only. Always follow your local formulary, national guidance, and independent prescribing standards.
