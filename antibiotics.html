Antibiotic prescribing in primary care

Use antibiotics only when there is a likely bacterial infection, when benefits outweigh harms, and when the narrowest effective option can be chosen. The aim is not just to treat infection, but to treat it safely, appropriately, and with good antimicrobial stewardship.

✅ Check allergy status
✅ Think likely organism
✅ Use the narrowest sensible option
✅ Check renal function / pregnancy
✅ Review local / NICE guidance
✅ Safety-net and escalate if red flags

Why choose one antibiotic over another?

Choice depends on the likely site of infection, likely bacteria, severity, patient factors, allergy status, pregnancy, renal function, previous cultures, and local resistance patterns.

Think stewardship

Broader-spectrum antibiotics are not “better” by default. Use the narrowest effective drug whenever possible to reduce resistance, adverse effects, and C. difficile risk.

Common prescribing mistake

Choosing an antibiotic because it “covers more” rather than because it best fits the likely organism and clinical scenario.

Escalate urgently if needed

Systemic toxicity, sepsis features, rapidly progressive infection, immunosuppression, deep space infection, or failure of appropriate oral treatment may require urgent review or admission.

Quick prescribing principles

Simple, punchy rules to guide antibiotic choice

1. Is it likely bacterial?

Many coughs, sore throats, sinus symptoms, and viral-type illnesses do not need antibiotics. Prescribing “just in case” causes harm and increases resistance.

2. Where is the infection?

Antibiotics are chosen by source: chest, urine, skin, dental, ENT, abdominal, or genital infection all have different likely organisms and first-line options.

3. What bacteria are likely?

Think common pathogens for that syndrome. Cellulitis is different from UTI; tonsillitis is different from COPD exacerbation; dental infection is different from pyelonephritis.

4. Use the narrowest effective agent

If a narrower-spectrum antibiotic will do the job, it is usually preferred over a broader one. This supports stewardship and reduces collateral damage.

5. Patient factors matter

Always check drug allergy, renal impairment, liver disease, pregnancy, breastfeeding, QT risk, drug interactions, swallowing ability, and adherence likelihood.

6. Review and safety-net

Tell patients what improvement to expect, what red flags to watch for, and when to re-attend. Antibiotics are not the only management step.

Bacteria basics

The terms you actually need to understand prescribing decisions

Gram-positive bacteria

These have a thick peptidoglycan cell wall and no outer membrane. Common Gram-positive organisms include streptococci and staphylococci. Many skin, soft tissue, and throat infections involve Gram-positive bacteria.

Gram-negative bacteria

These have a thinner cell wall but also an outer membrane, which can make them harder for some antibiotics to penetrate. Common Gram-negative organisms include E. coli, Haemophilus influenzae, and Klebsiella.

Atypical bacteria

These are organisms not well covered by standard beta-lactam antibiotics because they lack a typical cell wall or behave differently. Examples include Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, and Legionella. This is one reason macrolides or doxycycline may be chosen.

Anaerobes

Anaerobic bacteria grow in low-oxygen environments. They are often relevant in dental infection, deep tissue infection, intra-abdominal infection, and some abscesses. Metronidazole is commonly chosen when anaerobic cover is needed.

Beta-lactam antibiotics

This group includes penicillins, cephalosporins, and other related antibiotics. They work by interfering with bacterial cell wall synthesis. They are widely used, but not all bacteria are equally susceptible.

Beta-lactamase

Beta-lactamase is an enzyme made by some bacteria that can break down beta-lactam antibiotics and make them ineffective. This is one reason drugs like co-amoxiclav can be useful: clavulanic acid helps inhibit some beta-lactamases.

Bactericidal vs bacteriostatic

Bactericidal antibiotics kill bacteria. Bacteriostatic antibiotics mainly stop bacteria multiplying, allowing the immune system to clear infection. In day-to-day primary care, the clinical context matters more than the label alone.

Resistance

Resistance means bacteria are no longer reliably inhibited or killed by an antibiotic. It can arise through mutation or gene transfer. Broader-spectrum use, repeated use, and unnecessary prescribing all increase selection pressure.

Penicillins

Narrow when possible, broader only when justified

Best thought of as: the backbone of many common antibiotic choices, especially when a beta-lactam is appropriate and a narrower option can be used.
Examples
  • Phenoxymethylpenicillin
  • Amoxicillin
  • Flucloxacillin
  • Co-amoxiclav
Why choose penicillins?
  • Commonly effective against susceptible community pathogens
  • Often first-line when guideline-supported
  • Can allow a relatively narrow and stewardship-friendly approach
Why choose one penicillin over another?
  • Phenoxymethylpenicillin: narrower streptococcal cover
  • Amoxicillin: broader community use in selected ENT / respiratory settings
  • Flucloxacillin: better anti-staphylococcal activity
  • Co-amoxiclav: broader polymicrobial / beta-lactamase scenarios
Main cautions
  • Penicillin allergy must be checked carefully
  • Broader options should not replace narrower options without reason
  • Co-amoxiclav carries higher stewardship and C. difficile concern
🩹

Flucloxacillin

Think staphylococcal skin and soft tissue infection

Why it stands out: flucloxacillin is usually chosen because you want better anti-staphylococcal cover than amoxicillin would provide.
Choose when
  • Cellulitis is suspected
  • Skin / soft tissue infection fits typical Gram-positive skin flora
  • You want focused anti-staphylococcal activity
Why choose it over amoxicillin?
  • Amoxicillin is not the best anti-staphylococcal option
  • Flucloxacillin better fits common skin pathogens
  • More targeted choice for many uncomplicated skin infections
When it may not be enough
  • If polymicrobial infection is likely
  • If anaerobes are relevant
  • If bite-related infection changes likely organism mix
Key prescribing point

Use it because the likely organism profile fits it — not just because the infection “looks bacterial”.

🛡️

Co-amoxiclav

Useful when broader cover is justified

Remember: co-amoxiclav is not “better” because it is broader. It is useful when its extra breadth actually matters.
Why choose it?
  • Broader spectrum than plain amoxicillin
  • Contains clavulanic acid to inhibit some beta-lactamases
  • Useful when polymicrobial infection is more likely
Clinical situations where it may be chosen
  • Bite wounds
  • Selected dental infections
  • Some more complex or mixed infections if guidance supports it
Why avoid unnecessary use?
  • Broader-spectrum antibiotics increase resistance pressure
  • Higher collateral damage to normal flora
  • Higher risk of C. difficile than narrower choices
Punchy rule

If amoxicillin or another narrower agent would do, co-amoxiclav is often not the right first choice.

🫁

Macrolides

Penicillin-allergy alternative and atypical cover

Main reason to choose: a true penicillin allergy, or the need for atypical organism cover.
Examples
  • Clarithromycin
  • Erythromycin
  • Azithromycin
Why choose them?
  • Useful alternative to penicillins
  • Can cover atypical respiratory pathogens
  • Commonly used in respiratory and ENT settings where guidance supports it
When they may be preferred over penicillins
  • Documented penicillin allergy
  • Atypical infection felt more likely
  • Guidance specifically recommends them
Main cautions
  • QT prolongation risk
  • Drug interactions
  • GI upset
  • Resistance can limit usefulness
🌤️

Doxycycline

Useful breadth, especially where atypicals matter

Why choose doxycycline? It gives useful cover in a range of community scenarios and is especially helpful where atypicals are relevant.
Common reasons to choose it
  • Respiratory infection where guideline-supported
  • Penicillin allergy alternative
  • Atypical organism cover
  • Acne or Lyme disease
Why choose it over a macrolide?
  • Often convenient dosing
  • Good atypical activity
  • Can be a useful alternative if macrolide interaction / QT concerns exist
Main cautions
  • Pregnancy cautions
  • Not routinely suitable for young children in many scenarios
  • Photosensitivity
  • Oesophageal irritation if taken incorrectly
Punchy rule

Doxycycline is often chosen because it covers the right organisms in the right context — not just because it is “broad”.

🦷

Metronidazole

Think anaerobes

Main reason to choose metronidazole: you think anaerobic bacteria are important.
Common scenarios
  • Dental infection
  • Bacterial vaginosis
  • Intra-abdominal / anaerobic infection
  • Combined regimens where anaerobic cover is needed
Why choose it over others?
  • Excellent anaerobic activity
  • Especially useful when oxygen-poor tissue infection is suspected
  • Can complement another antibiotic if mixed cover is required
Why it may not be enough alone
  • May not adequately cover relevant aerobic bacteria
  • Some infections need dual coverage, not just anaerobic cover
Main cautions
  • Alcohol advice
  • Metallic taste
  • Neuropathy risk with prolonged use
🚽

Nitrofurantoin

Focused lower UTI treatment

Main reason to choose: likely lower UTI with no features suggesting upper UTI or sepsis, and renal function is adequate.
Why choose nitrofurantoin?
  • Strong first-line option for uncomplicated cystitis
  • Focused urinary activity
  • More stewardship-friendly than broader agents
Why choose it over trimethoprim?
  • Empiric resistance patterns often favour nitrofurantoin
  • A strong default first-line option where guidance supports it
Do not choose if...
  • Pyelonephritis is suspected
  • The patient is systemically unwell
  • Renal function is inadequate
Punchy rule

Nitrofurantoin is for the bladder, not the kidney.

🧫

Trimethoprim

Selected use rather than automatic first choice

Best used when: susceptibility is known or local guidance supports its use for lower UTI.
Why choose it?
  • Can be effective in susceptible lower UTI
  • Sometimes used when prior culture supports it
Why it may not be preferred empirically
  • Resistance can be higher than with nitrofurantoin
  • Not always the best “default” first-line choice
Main cautions
  • Hyperkalaemia risk
  • Renal impairment considerations
  • Drug interactions, including methotrexate concerns
Punchy rule

Trimethoprim is often a susceptibility-led choice, not a reflex choice.

📘

Cephalosporins

Useful drugs, but use should be justified

Stewardship point: cephalosporins can be valuable, but broader-spectrum antibiotics should not be used casually in primary care.
Why choose them?
  • Selected infections where guidance supports them
  • Useful when a cephalosporin’s spectrum fits the likely organism
Why caution is needed
  • Broader ecological impact
  • Increased stewardship concern
  • C. difficile risk can be higher than with narrower choices
Primary care view

Think of these as selected-use antibiotics, not everyday routine first-line options.

Key message

If a narrower, safer, guideline-supported antibiotic is enough, that is usually the better choice.

⚠️

Quinolones

Good activity, but important safety and stewardship concerns

High-yield message: quinolones are not casual “strong antibiotics”. Their risks and stewardship implications matter.
Why they may be chosen
  • Specific infections where guideline or specialist advice supports them
  • Situations where alternative options are unsuitable
Why they are used cautiously
  • Important adverse effect profile
  • Collateral damage and resistance concerns
  • Not usually a first reflex choice in routine primary care
Practical view

Use only when there is a good reason — not because they seem “powerful”.

Key message

Stronger stewardship concern usually means you should pause and ask whether a safer first-line option exists.

🏥

Hospital / specialist agents

Usually not routine first-line primary care prescribing

Examples: aminoglycosides, glycopeptides, carbapenems, and oxazolidinones such as linezolid.
Why they matter to know about
  • You may see them in discharge letters or microbiology advice
  • They are often used for resistant or severe infection
  • They help explain escalation pathways
Why they are not routine primary care choices
  • Need monitoring, IV delivery, or specialist oversight
  • Used for more resistant or severe pathogens
  • Higher-risk prescribing environment
Examples of what they cover
  • Vancomycin: resistant Gram-positive infection
  • Gentamicin: serious Gram-negative infection
  • Meropenem: very broad-spectrum severe infection
  • Linezolid: resistant Gram-positive infection
Primary care takeaway

Recognise them, but think of them mainly as hospital / specialist tools rather than standard primary care prescribing options.
