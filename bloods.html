Blood Test Interpretation

Foundations for new clinicians in primary care

Clinical Interpretation Loop (KISS)

  • Context: why was it taken? symptoms? meds? hydration? alcohol?
  • Pattern: interpret as systems (FBC / U&E / LFT pattern).
  • Trend: new vs chronic; rising vs stable.
  • Action: repeat, investigate, treat, refer, or urgent escalation.

Blood tests refine probability — they rarely create diagnoses.

Interpret Bloods as Groups (Pattern Recognition)

In primary care you rarely interpret a single number. Use “panel logic”: pattern + context + trend → action.

Suspected Heart Failure: U&E + BNP/NT-proBNP

Why together: BNP supports diagnosis; U&E tells you if kidneys/electrolytes can tolerate diuretics/ACEi/ARB/MRA.
Patterns: high BNP + symptoms → HF more likely; worsening creatinine / low sodium can indicate severity/diuretic effect.
Next step: compare baseline U&E, review diuretics/RAAS meds, consider ECG/CXR/echo pathway.

AKI vs CKD: U&E + Trend + Urine ACR

Why together: one creatinine is a photo; trend tells the story. ACR helps stage/prognosis for CKD.
Patterns: sudden rise vs baseline → AKI; persistent reduction ≥3 months → CKD.
Next step: meds (NSAIDs/ACEi/diuretics), hydration, repeat timing by severity; add ACR/BP; CKD framework if persistent.

Microcytic Anaemia: FBC (Hb/MCV) + Ferritin (+ CRP)

Why together: ferritin confirms iron deficiency; CRP helps if ferritin is falsely “normal” in inflammation.
Patterns: low Hb + low MCV + low ferritin → iron deficiency; normal/high ferritin + raised CRP can still be iron-restricted.
Next step: source of blood loss (GI/menstrual), start replacement per pathway, recheck Hb response.

Macrocytosis: FBC (MCV) + B12 + Folate + LFTs + TSH

Why together: macrocytosis is “multi-cause”; these are the common reversible primary care causes.
Patterns: low B12/folate → deficiency; raised LFTs/alcohol history → hepatic/alcohol; high TSH → hypothyroid.
Next step: treat deficiency (don’t miss B12), address alcohol/thyroid, repeat FBC to confirm response.

Cholestatic LFTs: ALP + Bilirubin (+ GGT if available)

Why together: distinguishes cholestatic pattern vs hepatocellular pattern; helps decide imaging urgency.
Patterns: ALP ± bilirubin rise suggests cholestasis; bilirubin + symptoms (itch/jaundice) raises concern.
Next step: review meds/alcohol; repeat if mild; if persistent/symptomatic → ultrasound / hepatobiliary pathway.

Metabolic Syndrome Snapshot: HbA1c + Lipids + ALT

Why together: diabetes risk + atherogenic lipids + fatty liver often travel together.
Patterns: rising HbA1c + high TG/low HDL + raised ALT suggests insulin resistance/metabolic risk.
Next step: lifestyle plan, CV risk management (BP/lipids), NAFLD pathway if persistent LFT rise.

Inflammation: CRP + FBC (WCC/neutrophils) + Clinical Picture

Why together: CRP alone is non-specific; pairing with FBC and symptoms improves meaning.
Patterns: neutrophilia + high CRP fits bacterial inflammation; lymphocytosis can fit viral; normal CRP doesn’t exclude early illness.
Next step: treat the patient; repeat only if it changes management or safety-netting requires it.

Statin Safety: Lipids + Baseline LFTs (+ CK only if symptomatic)

Why together: lipids show benefit; LFTs provide baseline. CK is not routine unless symptoms/high risk.
Patterns: improved non-HDL/LDL suggests adherence/response; new symptoms guide CK decision.
Next step: reinforce adherence, side-effect check, repeat lipids per pathway.

Tip: Always look for a pattern that answers your question: diagnosis support, severity, treatment safety, or monitoring response.

Focused Tests: When to order + Treatment + Monitoring

Clinician-facing summary. Medication choices and follow-up intervals vary by local policy/NICE and patient factors.

Ferritin / Iron Deficiency

When to request

Low Hb and/or low MCV • fatigue + risk factors • suspected blood loss • unexplained thrombocytosis

Common causes

Menstrual loss • GI loss • diet • malabsorption • pregnancy/postpartum

Treatment (typical)

Oral iron replacement (ferrous salt) if tolerated • treat underlying cause • consider IV iron via pathway if severe, intolerance, or poor response

Monitoring

Repeat Hb to confirm response • continue replacement beyond normalisation to replenish stores (per pathway) • if non-response: check adherence, ongoing loss, alternative diagnosis

Safety / escalation

Symptomatic anaemia, very low Hb, or red flag GI symptoms → urgent assessment / referral per pathway

Vitamin B12

When to request

Macrocytosis • neuropathy/paraesthesia/gait change • cognitive change • glossitis • dietary/malabsorption risk • metformin/PPIs long-term

Common causes

Dietary deficiency • pernicious anaemia • malabsorption (GI disease/surgery)

Treatment (typical)

Replace B12 (IM hydroxocobalamin or oral therapy depending on cause/severity/local guidance) • treat urgently if neurological features suspected

Monitoring

Symptom response is key • repeat FBC ± B12 per local schedule • consider long-term plan if pernicious anaemia

Safety

Neurological symptoms with suspected deficiency = don’t delay treatment while waiting for repeat tests

Folate

When to request

Macrocytosis/anaemia • malnutrition • alcohol excess • malabsorption risk • pregnancy context (per pathway)

Critical point

Check/consider B12 when folate is low — folate alone can mask B12 deficiency while neuro damage progresses

Treatment (typical)

Folic acid replacement if confirmed deficiency • address underlying cause (diet/alcohol/malabsorption/meds)

Monitoring

Repeat FBC to confirm response • review ongoing risk factors • ensure B12 deficiency excluded/treated

HbA1c

When to request

Hyperglycaemia symptoms • incidental raised glucose • risk factors • monitoring diabetes/prediabetes

What it means

Average glycaemia over ~2–3 months. Use plasma glucose if you need an acute “right now” answer

Treatment (typical)

Lifestyle intervention for prediabetes/early disease • medication choice depends on diagnosis, CV/renal status and local guidance (metformin is commonly first-line if appropriate)

Monitoring

Repeat HbA1c per pathway • monitor renal function, BP, lipids and complications screening • review adherence/side effects if on therapy

Safety

Marked symptoms/very high glucose features → same-day assessment for hyperglycaemic emergency per pathway

Lipids

When to request

CV risk assessment • diabetes • hypertension • CKD • metabolic syndrome/NAFLD • before statin • strong FHx/premature CVD

Fasting vs non-fasting

Non-fasting often OK for risk scoring. Consider fasting if TG raised, suspected familial dyslipidaemia, or follow-up clarity

Treatment (typical)

Lifestyle first for most • statin for primary prevention based on risk • statin is standard for secondary prevention per pathway (e.g., atorvastatin class)

Monitoring

Baseline LFTs before statin • repeat lipids after starting/changing therapy per pathway • check adherence + muscle symptoms; CK only if symptomatic/high risk

When to suspect FH

Markedly raised LDL/total • premature CVD in patient/1st-degree relatives • tendon xanthomas/early corneal arcus → follow FH pathway

Targets by Risk (Quick Panel)

These are common “teaching” targets. Always use your local/NICE pathway for exact thresholds and targets.

Lower risk / general reference

Non-HDL: often < 4.0 mmol/L • Total: < 5.0 mmol/L • TG: < 1.7 mmol/L

Primary prevention (higher risk)

Use a risk tool + shared decision-making. Targets are typically more stringent than general reference, often focusing on non-HDL/LDL reduction rather than one “magic number”.

Secondary prevention (established CVD)

Aim for aggressive lipid lowering (statin standard). Targets are usually lower than primary prevention; follow the local secondary prevention pathway.

Diabetes / CKD / very high risk

Often treated similarly to high/secondary-risk groups depending on age, albuminuria, and comorbidities. Focus on non-HDL/LDL lowering + global CV risk.

Common Primary Care Pitfalls

  • Ordering broad panels without a clinical question (noise → over-investigation).
  • Reacting to mild isolated abnormalities without repeat/confirmation.
  • Ignoring hydration, illness, and medication effects.
  • Not checking historical baseline (AKI vs CKD; chronic LFT rise, etc.).
